Exploring the Relationship Between Emissions and Climate Disaster Frequency

Motivation

The visualization helps the veiwer explore how long-term climate shifts relate to short-term disasters. Presenting these datasets together allows us to see if patterns that are not visible when each is viewed individually, without suggesting a direct relationship.

Design Rationale

We use a stacked bar chart to show the total number of disasters per year while also revealing how different disaster types contribute to that total. A line chart is overlaid using a secondary y-axis to show CO₂ emissions, providing a continuous trend for comparison. Disaster types have a distinct categorical color palette, while the CO₂ line is styled in a neutral charcoal with a light shadow so it remains legible across bars. Interactive controls like region selection, sorting, and disaster-type filtering aid in the exploratory analysis.

Conclusion

Although CO₂ emissions show a steady long-term increase, climate-related disaster counts vary significantly from year to year, and do not directly track changes in emissions on an annual basis. This suggests that emissions only influence climate risks over larger time scales, while individual natural disaster events are shaped by shorter term environmental and regional factors.
